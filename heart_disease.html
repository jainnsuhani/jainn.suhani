A tool aimed at reducing the mortality rate associated with heart disease

In 2021, 17.9 million people worldwide died from cardiovascular disease, making it the number one cause of death of people worldwide. Cardiovascular disease includes diseases like coronary heart disease, heart attacks and strokes. Developing a tool that allows individuals to assess their likelihood of developing cardiovascular disease based on their lifestyle choices can significantly contribute to reducing the number of deaths caused by cardiovascular issues. The heart disease dataset from the University of California, Irvine contains processed information about Cleveland patients at different stages of heart disease, ranging from absence,0 , to severe, 4. Each patient's record includes various variables such as age, gender, fasting blood sugar levels, and more. By leveraging this dataset, we aim to create a classifier that utilizes measurements of cardiovascular disease risk factors, such as cholesterol levels, to predict the future occurrence of heart disease in others. Therefore, we are posing the question, can we utilize the measurements of known risk factors from individuals with heart disease to predict the presence of heart disease in new observations?

We will cover data preparation, processing, splitting, model training, and predictions using R packages. We will focus on risk factors such as Cholesterol Level, Age, Fasting Blood Sugar, and Blood Pressure. We will clean the dataset by handling missing values, removing irrelevant columns, and ensuring appropriate data types. We will split the data into training and testing subsets using functions like ‘InitialSplit’ and ‘SetSeed’. We will train the model using the selected risk factor variables as predictors and the target variable (presence/absence and severity of heart disease). We will apply the trained model to new observations to predict heart disease presence. We will use R packages like ‘tidyverse’ for data manipulation and ‘ggplot’ for data visualization. We will summarize the data using summary statistics and visualize it using bar and scatter plots. By cleaning, summarizing, and visualizing the heart disease dataset, we will explore potential relationships between variables and gain a better understanding of the data distribution. These steps will provide a foundation for further analysis and modeling to predict heart disease presence and severity based on risk factor measurements.

The dataset includes variables such as age, cholesterol level, fasting blood sugar, and blood pressure, which are known risk factors for cardiovascular disease. By examining the data, we can identify the extent to which each risk factor is associated with the presence and severity of heart disease. Exploring the dataset may also reveal patterns and correlations between different variables.

Analyzing the heart dataset can lead to a deeper understanding of the relationship between risk factors and heart disease, uncover important insights, and potentially help in developing effective strategies for prevention and treatment of cardiovascular diseases. The findings from analyzing the dataset can lead to early identification of heart diseases and the implementation of preventive measures such as lifestyle modification or medication. Clinicians can tailor treatment plans to each patient's specific needs by considering individual risk factors.

Future research could explore the prognosis, survival rates, and factors influencing disease progression in these patients. By pursuing these future questions, we can expand our knowledge of heart disease and improve risk assessments and management strategies. Lastly, the use of this classifier raises the question, are other variables, not widely known risk factors of heart disease, that are found in this dataset able to accurately predict the occurrence of heart disease?
